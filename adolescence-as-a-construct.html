2

What is adolescence? The Western idea

10 min

In most Western societies, adolescence is seen as a distinct stage of life — roughly ages 12 to 18 — where a person is no longer a child but not yet fully an adult. It is associated with identity exploration, increased independence, peer relationships, and emotional change.

Key idea

A social construct is something that exists because a society agrees it exists — not because it is biologically inevitable. Last lesson we discussed how identity is socially constructed. Today the question is: is adolescence itself a social construct?

The concept of adolescence as we know it is relatively recent. Before the 20th century in Western societies, children often moved directly into adult roles — working, marrying, taking on responsibilities — with no recognised "in between" stage.

Psychologist G. Stanley Hall was the first to formally define adolescence as a developmental stage in 1904. Since then it has become a central part of Western culture — but is it universal?

Think about it

Can you think of a moment in your own life where you were treated "in between" — not quite a child, not quite an adult? What was that like?

3

Adolescence across cultures — four examples

15 min

Read each of the four cultural examples below. For each one, think about whether it fits the Western idea of adolescence — or challenges it.

Indigenous Australian — initiation

In many Aboriginal cultures, the transition from childhood to adulthood is marked by specific ceremonies and initiations — not a prolonged "in between" stage. Adult responsibilities and knowledge are conferred at a defined point, often in early adolescence.

Japan — Seijin-no-Hi

Coming of Age Day (成人の日) marks the transition to adulthood at age 20 with a national ceremony. Young people dress in traditional clothing and are officially welcomed into adult society — a clear cultural marker rather than a gradual transition.

Amish — Rumspringa

Amish teenagers enter a period called Rumspringa (roughly ages 14–21) where they are permitted to explore the outside world before choosing whether to be baptised into the Amish church. A sanctioned period of exploration — but with a defined endpoint.

Rural communities globally

In many rural and agricultural communities across Asia, Africa, and South America, children take on significant adult labour and family responsibilities from a young age. The Western concept of a protected, school-focused adolescence does not apply in the same way.

4

Adolescence as a social construct — the argument

10 min

Now that you have looked at the four cultural examples, read the argument below carefully.

The argument

Adolescence is not a biological inevitability — it is a social construct created by Western, industrialised societies. It emerged when compulsory schooling delayed entry into the workforce, creating a prolonged period of economic dependence. Cultures without these conditions do not produce adolescence in the same form. Therefore, adolescence is culturally specific, not universal.

This does not mean that puberty is a social construct — biological changes at this age are universal. The argument is that the social experience of adolescence — the "in between" status, the identity exploration, the dependence on parents — is shaped by culture, not biology.
